Game Design Research

Studio 3.4 ~ March 2, 2023

Games have a lot of interactions but I never really considered the complexity associated with every UI decision. Certain games have specific orientation they have to be played in but I never really thought about reach able areas. I learned that certain parts of the screen are easier to reach than others depending on the orientation that is being used. The games are usually also designed for people who use their right hand so this may create added difficulty for those who are left handed. Additionally, typically when I see popups, I look for the "X" as I want to get rid of the content which designers found is the same for many users. As a result, game designers are now looking to use alternatives like done or close positioned at the bottom of the overlay instead of using one of the top corners. Overall, I will keep this best practices in mind when designing my own game, "Game On".

Overuse of Overlays: How to Avoid Misusing Lightboxes

Studio 1.3 ~ Febuary 7, 2023

An overlay is typically a small box displayed on top of a portion of a page. The apple example of bad overlay use is interesting as we don't really notice how common this technique really is. Many sites that are geared towards helping solve some issues have scrollable overlays trying to fit as much information as possible into a mall window. As a designer, I will provide a way to close the overlay as I have experienced this frustration as a user where I have to wait for a certain amount of time to be able to close it or spend some time looking for the button to close it as it's not easy to find.

When considering the use of overlays, I will take into account my target audience, their intended task, the possibility of causing an interruption, where the window will be positioned, and understand my decision to make an overlay instead of a page. I want to be able to use overlays without creating confusion and limiting accessibility.

Best Practices for Form Design

Studio 1.1 ~ January 31, 2023

As designers, we have to account for the amount of work users want to put into forms. For example, when filling out surveys, I look to see how long it is before deciding if I am to complete it at the moment or come back to it later. I agree with the "less is more" theory. Simplifying the form whether that's putting everything within one column or categorizing sections will help users have a faster completion time. I don't like filling out forms that feel endless. Some don't include any progress bar or indication of the survey coming to an end which diminishes my experience.

Additionally, it helps to differentiate any requirements whether that's special formatting or marking required fields. Previously, I would use placeholder text but now I realize this isn't the best way to provide hints as the text disappears once the user starts typing something.
